Getting Started With Investing at 18 — What I Wish I Knew

John · 28 Apr 2026 · 6 min read

Most people I know my age aren't thinking about investing. They're thinking about what to do on the weekend, what job they might want, whether they'll pass their exams. And I get it — when you've got £200 in your account, the stock market feels like something for people with actual money.

But here's the thing nobody told me until I went looking: time is the one advantage young people have over literally everyone else. And you can't buy more of it later.

Why starting at 18 matters more than people think

Compound interest is one of those things that sounds boring until you actually run the numbers. If you invest £100 a month from age 18 with a 7% average annual return, by the time you're 65 you'd have over £370,000. If you wait until 28 to start the exact same habit, you'd end up with around £186,000. Same monthly investment, half the outcome — just because you waited 10 years.

The best time to start was yesterday. The second best time is today, even with £50.

What I actually did

I opened a Trading 212 account. It's free, there are no trading fees on stocks, and you can start with literally £1. I put in a small amount each month — not because I'm rolling in cash, I'm not — but because the habit matters more than the amount right now.

My main position is in Rocket Lab (RKLB). That's a more aggressive bet than I'd recommend for most people starting out. If I was doing it again, I'd probably start with a broad index fund — something like the S&P 500 or a global ETF — before moving into individual stocks.

The stuff I wish someone had told me earlier

  • An ISA (Stocks & Shares ISA) lets you invest up to £20,000 a year with zero tax on gains. Use one.
  • Index funds beat most actively managed funds over the long term. This is not controversial — the data is clear.
  • Don't check your portfolio every day. You will make bad decisions based on short-term noise.
  • Investing while in debt with high interest rates (credit cards, etc.) is usually a bad idea — clear those first.

None of this is financial advice. I'm 18, not a financial advisor. But this is what I've learned so far, and if even one person reads this and starts investing a year earlier than they would have, that's worth something.
